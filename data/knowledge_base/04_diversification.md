# Diversification

Diversification is the only "free lunch" in investing — it reduces unsystematic
(idiosyncratic) risk without reducing expected return. It does *not* protect against
systematic (market-wide) risk.

## Across what dimensions?

1. **Securities** — owning the market via a low-cost index fund eliminates
   single-stock risk. A typical broad-market fund holds 500–4,000 names.
2. **Sectors and styles** — a portfolio dominated by one sector (e.g., tech)
   carries concentration risk even if it holds 50 names.
3. **Geography** — US equities are ~60% of global market cap. A US-only portfolio
   misses 40% of the world's investable equity. International developed and
   emerging markets have low (but not zero) correlation to US.
4. **Asset classes** — stocks and high-quality bonds have historically had
   correlation near zero or slightly negative during recessions, providing
   ballast.
5. **Time** — dollar-cost averaging spreads entry risk over many price points,
   though lump-sum investing has a higher expected return when you have the cash
   today.

## Anti-patterns

- **Diworsification** — owning 10 large-cap US equity funds is not diversification;
  they all hold the same names.
- **Home bias** — investors globally over-weight their domestic market by 2–3×
  market cap. Some bias is reasonable (currency match, lower fees) but extreme
  home bias raises risk for no extra expected return.
- **Concentration in employer stock** — common via stock options/RSUs but doubles
  the bet: if the employer fails, both the job and the savings vanish. Cap
  employer stock at <10% of net worth.
