# Asset Allocation Basics

Asset allocation is the practice of dividing an investment portfolio across the three
broad asset classes — equities (stocks), fixed income (bonds), and cash equivalents —
to balance expected return against risk.

## Why allocation matters

Empirical studies (Brinson, Hood, and Beebower 1986; Ibbotson and Kaplan 2000) suggest
that more than 90% of the variation in long-run portfolio returns is explained by
asset allocation rather than security selection or market timing. Allocation is the
single most important decision an investor makes after deciding to save at all.

## Common allocation rules of thumb

- **"100 minus age" in equities** — a starting point that decreases stock exposure as
  the investor ages. Modern variants use 110 or 120 minus age to reflect longer
  lifespans.
- **Conservative target**: 30–40% equities, 50–60% bonds, 5–15% cash. Suitable for
  capital preservation and near-term income needs (retirees with a 5–10 year horizon).
- **Moderate target**: 60–70% equities, 25–35% bonds, 5% cash. Suitable for
  mid-career investors saving for retirement or college.
- **Aggressive target**: 80–95% equities (with international and small-cap tilts),
  5–15% bonds/cash. Suitable for investors with 15+ year horizons and a high
  tolerance for drawdowns.

## Rebalancing

Allocations drift as markets move. Rebalance back to target either on a schedule
(annually or semi-annually) or when an asset class is more than 5 percentage points
off its target. Rebalancing in a tax-advantaged account avoids capital gains taxes.

## Sub-allocation within equities

A balanced equity sleeve typically includes:
- 60–70% domestic (e.g., total US stock market)
- 25–35% international developed markets
- 5–10% emerging markets
- Optional small-cap and value tilts for factor exposure
