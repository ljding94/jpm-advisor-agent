# Tax-Advantaged Accounts

Using the right account types is one of the highest-leverage decisions in personal
finance — often worth more than security selection over a lifetime.

## US account types

### Pre-tax (deductible now, taxed at withdrawal)

- **401(k) / 403(b)** — employer-sponsored. 2025 limit: $23,500 for under-50,
  $31,000 with catch-up at 50+. Match captures should always come first; an
  employer match is a 50–100% guaranteed return.
- **Traditional IRA** — $7,000 limit ($8,000 at 50+). Deduction phases out at high
  incomes if covered by a workplace plan.

### Roth (taxed now, tax-free at withdrawal)

- **Roth IRA** — same dollar limits as Traditional IRA, with income phase-outs.
  Best for low-tax-bracket years (early career, sabbaticals, retirement gaps).
- **Roth 401(k)** — same limits as traditional 401(k); no income limits. Many
  employers now offer both.
- **Backdoor Roth** — high earners can contribute to a non-deductible Traditional
  IRA and immediately convert to Roth. Requires care to avoid the *pro-rata rule*
  if other pre-tax IRA assets exist.

### HSA (triple-tax-advantaged)

- Contributions deductible, growth tax-free, withdrawals tax-free if used for
  qualified medical expenses.
- 2025 limit: $4,300 individual, $8,550 family.
- After 65, withdrawals for non-medical use are taxed like a Traditional IRA
  (but without the 20% penalty).
- Best treated as a stealth retirement account: pay current medical expenses out
  of pocket and let the HSA invest and compound.

## Order of operations (general)

1. Capture the full 401(k) match.
2. Pay off high-interest debt (>~7%).
3. Max HSA if eligible.
4. Max Roth or Traditional IRA (whichever fits the marginal tax-rate logic).
5. Max remaining 401(k) contribution.
6. Mega-backdoor Roth (if plan allows).
7. Taxable brokerage with tax-efficient funds.
