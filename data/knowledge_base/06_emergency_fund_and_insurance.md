# Emergency Funds and Insurance

Investment plans collapse when forced selling occurs at the wrong time. The
purpose of an emergency fund and insurance is to make forced selling unnecessary.

## Emergency fund sizing

- **Standard guidance**: 3–6 months of essential expenses in a high-yield savings
  account.
- **6–12 months** for: single-income households, commission/variable income,
  industries with long unemployment durations, anyone with health risks.
- **3 months** can be sufficient for: dual-income households in stable salaried
  jobs, ample short-term credit available, no dependents.

The emergency fund is *not* an investment. It must be liquid, principal-stable,
and accessible in 1–2 business days. Money-market funds and HYSAs are the right
home; bond funds (which can drop 10%+ in a rate-shock year) are not.

## Insurance — what to buy and why

- **Health** — non-negotiable. The single largest source of US bankruptcies is
  medical debt; an unfunded medical event can wipe out decades of savings.
- **Term life** — for anyone whose income or care others depend on. 10–20× annual
  income, level term to age 60–65. Whole/universal life is rarely cost-effective
  for ordinary investors.
- **Long-term disability** — replaces 60% of income if you can't work. The
  probability of a disability lasting 90+ days during a working life (30%) is
  higher than the probability of dying during it. Often available through
  employers at low cost.
- **Umbrella liability** — $1–2M of coverage for ~$200–400/yr; protects assets
  from lawsuits exceeding home/auto policy limits. Strongly recommended once
  net worth exceeds ~$500K.
- **Long-term care** — increasingly important for retirees with $500K–$2M of
  assets (under that, Medicaid is the safety net; over that, self-insurance is
  often cheaper than premiums).

## What *not* to buy

- Cancer/disease-specific policies (covered by health insurance).
- Credit card insurance.
- Most extended warranties.
- Whole life used as an investment vehicle (high fees, poor flexibility).
