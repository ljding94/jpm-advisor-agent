# Risk Tolerance Frameworks

Risk tolerance describes how much short-term loss an investor can stomach without
abandoning their long-term plan. It has two components:

- **Risk capacity** — the *objective* ability to absorb losses, set by income,
  savings, time horizon, and required spending.
- **Risk willingness** — the *subjective* psychological tolerance for volatility.

Both must be considered. A 28-year-old with a 35-year horizon has high capacity but
may have low willingness if they panic-sold in a previous crash. The lower of the two
should generally govern allocation.

## Categorical labels

- **Conservative** — accepts <10% drawdown; prioritizes capital preservation;
  suitable for short horizons (<5 years), retirees living off the portfolio, or
  investors with low willingness regardless of capacity.
- **Moderate** — accepts 15–25% drawdown for a higher long-term return; suitable
  for 10–20 year horizons.
- **Aggressive** — accepts 30–50% drawdown; suitable for 15+ year horizons and
  investors who have demonstrated emotional resilience through prior bear markets.

## Behavioral pitfalls

- *Recency bias* inflates risk willingness during bull markets and crushes it during
  bear markets.
- *Loss aversion* — the pain of a loss is roughly 2× the pleasure of an equivalent
  gain (Kahneman and Tversky 1979). Use this to set a stress-test number: "if your
  $500K portfolio dropped to $300K next year, what would you do?" If the honest
  answer is "sell," the allocation is too aggressive.
